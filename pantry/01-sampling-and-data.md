# Pantry — Chapter 1: Sampling and Data

## Scenes
- Literary Digest 1936 election prediction failure (cold open and integration): 10 million surveys sent, 2 million responses, predicted Landon over Roosevelt by 18 points—outcome catastrophically wrong. Shows why method beats size.
- Polling firm the night before election (Concept 1 open): 200 phone lines, 1,200 interviews, estimating candidate preference. Introduces parameter vs. statistic.
- Chronic pain medication trial (Concept 1 worked example): 500 patients randomized, 180/250 in medication group experience relief. Clinical context for population/sample/parameter/statistic.
- Hospital intake questions (Concept 2 open): Blood type, prior visits, pain scale—three different kinds of variables, three different things you can do with them.
- Meditation app anxiety test (Concept 3 open): 400 students randomly assigned, 200 to app/200 control, measures anxiety after 4 weeks. Introduction to randomized experiment.
- Company wellness program observational vs. experimental (Concept 3 worked example): Self-selected participants vs. random assignment; shows why causation requires randomization.

## Analogies
- Method as foundation, size as precision: "A large biased sample is worse than a small unbiased one." (No metaphor needed; the principle is clear.)
- Confounding variables as hidden factors: "The third variable—initial ability—affects both study hours and exam scores." (Clear mechanistic language, no extended analogy.)

## Etymologies
- **Population**, from Latin *populo* (the people): the entire group you care about.
- **Sample**, from Old French *essample* (example, specimen): a piece chosen to stand for the whole.
- **Parameter**, from Greek *para* (beside) + *metron* (measure): a fixed number describing the whole population, beside but distinct from any sample.
- **Statistic**, from Latin *status* (state, condition): a number describing the state or condition of a sample.
- **Categorical** or **qualitative**, from Latin *qualitas* (quality): data that describe a quality or category, not a quantity.
- **Quantitative**, from Latin *quantus* (how much): data that measure quantity—countable or measurable amounts.
- **Bias**, from Old French *biais* (slant): a systematic slant in one direction; when a sample is slanted toward one group in the population.
- **Confound**, from Latin *confundere* (to mix together, to confuse): a third variable mixed into the relationship between two variables, confusing causation with correlation.

## Trade-offs named
1. **Sampling method vs. sample size:** A small random sample is better than a huge biased one. Method determines reliability; size improves precision once method is sound.
2. **Speed vs. accuracy in sampling:** Random sampling is slow and expensive; convenience sampling is fast and cheap—but gives unreliable data. The company chooses based on consequences.
3. **Observational study vs. randomized experiment:** Observational studies are fast, cheap, and natural; experiments are expensive and complex but can prove causation. Choose based on what you need: insight or proof.
4. **Categorical vs. quantitative data:** Categorical data lose detail but are easy to categorize and count; quantitative data are rich and allow arithmetic but can obscure categories.

## Scale shifts used
1. One phone call → 1,200 interviews → state-wide estimate of voter preference.
2. Hospital nurse asking one patient → classifying all patients' characteristics.
3. 500 patients in medication trial → hope to generalize to millions of patients with the condition.
4. Four students' study hours and exam scores (observational) → 400 students randomized to meditation or control (experimental).

## Worked examples
1. Parameter/statistic in chronic pain trial: 250 patients in medication group, 180 experience relief, statistic = 180/250 = 0.72.
2. Classifying variables (study hours, major, exam score, sleep): discrete vs. continuous, categorical vs. quantitative.
3. Measurement scales (class year, GPA, housing satisfaction): ordinal vs. ratio.
4. Observational vs. randomized (wellness program, meditation app).

## Common misconceptions addressed (detailed)

**"A large sample is always reliable."** The Literary Digest had 2 million responses. Enormous. Useless. Biased toward the wealthy. No size fixes method.

**"Random means I choose whoever I feel like."** Random means a machine picks, not you. Your judgment is the enemy of randomness. A random number generator picks, not your gut.

**"If my sample is big enough, I don't need to worry about the method."** Wrong. Size improves precision; method determines whether the sample can represent the population at all. Biased method breaks everything.

**"All numbers are the same kind of data."** A pain scale (1-5) is ordinal (has order but unknown distances). A test score (0-100) is ratio (has zero and equal intervals). Different kinds demand different analyses.

**"I can always compute a mean."** Means of categorical data are nonsense. Means of ordinal data are debatable. Means work for interval and ratio data.

**"Correlation proves causation."** Correlation is an association. Causation means one variable directly affects the other. Confounders can create correlations without causation.

**"A big observational study is as good as a small experiment."** No. Method beats size for causal claims. A huge observational study cannot prove causation; a small, well-designed experiment can.
