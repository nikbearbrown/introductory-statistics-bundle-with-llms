**Figure 1.3: Types of Variables**
- A 2×2 grid:
  - **Categorical / Qualitative**: Blood type (A, B, AB, O), color, major
  - **Quantitative Discrete**: Number of visits (0, 1, 2, 3), number of books, count of siblings
  - **Quantitative Continuous**: Pain scale (0-10, can be 7.5), height in inches, temperature
- Annotation for each: what operations are valid (count only? add and average? measure across a range?)

**Figure 1.4: Measurement Scales**
- A vertical scale showing progression:
  - **Nominal**: Unordered categories (blood type: A, B, AB, O—no order)
  - **Ordinal**: Ordered categories (satisfaction: unsatisfied, satisfied, very satisfied—order but no defined distance)
  - **Interval**: Ordered numbers with equal intervals, no true zero (temperature: 0°C is not absence of heat)
  - **Ratio**: Ordered numbers with equal intervals and true zero (height, salary, test score)
- Annotation: "What arithmetic is allowed?"

**Figure 1.5: Observational Study vs. Randomized Experiment**
- Left column (Observational): 
  - Population → Self-selected sample (volunteers) → Split into groups (group 1: volunteers in wellness program; group 2: non-volunteers)
  - Outcome: Can observe that group 1 takes fewer sick days
  - Problem: Initial health/conscientiousness is a confounder
- Right column (Randomized Experiment):
  - Population → Random sample → Random assignment to groups (group A: wellness; group B: control)
  - Outcome: Group A takes fewer sick days
  - Advantage: Random assignment balances confounders across groups; difference is caused by the program

**Figure 1.6: Confounding Variables**
- A Venn diagram with three overlapping circles:
  - Circle 1: "Study hours" (explanatory variable)
  - Circle 2: "Exam score" (response variable)
  - Circle 3: "Prior ability" (confounder)
- Overlaps show: Ability affects both study hours (high-ability students may study less efficiently) and exam scores (high-ability students score higher)
- Caption: "Ability is confounded with study hours; cannot isolate the effect of study hours on exam performance from observation alone."

**Figure 1.7: Sample Methods Comparison**
- A table comparing:
  - **Simple Random**: Every member has equal chance; unbiased; slow/expensive
  - **Stratified**: Population divided into strata; proportional random sample from each; unbiased; ensures representation
  - **Systematic**: Every nth person from list; fast; works if list has no pattern
  - **Cluster**: Random clusters selected, all members included; convenient; risky if clusters not representative
  - **Convenience**: Easiest people to reach; fast/cheap; biased

## Notes on visualization

The chapter is heavily narrative and conceptual. Most figures support understanding of method rather than displaying statistical results. Figures 1.2 (biased vs. unbiased) and 1.5 (observational vs. experimental) are essential; they make abstract concepts concrete by side-by-side comparison.

Figure 1.6 (Venn diagram of confounding) is powerful for showing why observational studies cannot prove causation—the visual makes clear why you cannot isolate one variable when a third is tangled with both.

No data tables or histograms are introduced in this chapter; those belong to Chapter 2 (descriptive statistics). This chapter is about *where* data come from and *why* method matters, not about displaying or summarizing data once you have it.
